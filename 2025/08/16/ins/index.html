Question background

Regardless of resistance, what curve takes the shortest time for a point to move from one point to another in a vertical plane? In 1697, John Bernoulli provided the solution to the steepest descent line problem.
Now there is a circle, and at any point on it, when the circle rolls purely once on the ground, the trajectory of this point is called a cycloid. Cycloid is also the solution to the problem of the steepest descent line.

Deduction of Parameter Equations

$$v = \frac{ds}{dt} $$

$$ds = \sqrt{1+y^{'2}} dx $$

$$v = \sqrt{2gy} $$

$$dt = \frac{ds}{v} $$

$$t_{min}=\int_0^{x_0}{\frac{\sqrt{1+y^{'2}}}{\sqrt{2gy}}}dx $$

The solution using variational method is：

$$x=c(\theta-sin\theta) $$

$$y=c(1-cos\theta) $$

The coefficient is determined by the target coordinates

Thought questions1

Question: What curve does a homogeneous disk with a radius of R roll purely along, and does it descend the fastest?
Provide the equation for the “steepest descent line” of a disk.

The kinetic energy is the sum of translational kinetic energy and rotational kinetic energy：

$$T=\frac{1}{2}mv^2+\frac{1}{2}Jw^2 $$

and $J=\frac{1}{2}mR^2$, pure rolling condition$v=wR$，we can get

$$T=\frac{3}{4}mv^2 $$

so $v=\sqrt{\frac{4}{3}gy}$

$$t_{min}=\int_0^{x_0}{\frac{\sqrt{1+y^{'2}}}{\sqrt{\frac{4}{3} gy}}}dx $$

Due to the only difference in coefficients, using the same variational method as the first part of the derivation, the result obtained is still a cycloid.Set the cycloid with a downward direction as the center trajectory, and set the parameter equation to the following form:：

$$x=R(\theta-sin\theta) $$

$$y=-R(1-cos\theta) $$

In order to obtain the true trajectory curve, that is, the disk descends along such a curve, a method is first proposed, which calculates the normal direction of each point of the cycloid at the center of the circle, and extends the length of a radius in that direction, which is the desired curve. This method has no mathematical expression and can be used to compare the calculated trajectory with the theoretical trajectory in the animation
Now derive the theoretical trajectory. The angle between the cycloid tangent of the center trajectory and the horizontal line is $\gamma $ . First, calculate the tangent

$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy}{d\theta}\frac{d\theta}{dx}=-\frac{sin\theta}{1-cos\theta} $$

Assuming $\gamma $ is an acute angle, then

$$tan\gamma=\frac{sin\theta}{1-cos\theta} $$

obtain

$$sin\gamma=\frac{sin\theta}{\sqrt{2-2cos\theta}}=cos\frac{\theta}{2} $$

$$cos\gamma=\frac{1-cos\theta}{2sin\frac{\theta}{2}}=sin\frac{\theta}{2} $$

Let the coordinates of the desired trajectory line be $x^{'},y^{'}$

$$x^{'}=x-Rsin\gamma $$

$$y^{'}=y-Rcos\gamma $$

obtain

$$x^{'}=R(\theta-sin\theta-cos\frac{\theta}{2}) $$

$$y^{'}=-R(1-cos\theta+sin\frac{\theta}{2}) $$

This parameter equation is the required curve equation. Note that the parameters of the cycloid equation for the center of the circle can be set according to the target point. Setting the above derivation as $R$ is only for convenience. The program simulates the surface calculation trajectory to coincide with the theoretical trajectory, which has been demonstrated in the animation
To ensure the authenticity of the animation demonstration, a derivation of $\theta$ with respect to time $t$ is made. Firstly, the parameter equation is assumed to be as follows:

$$x=R(\theta-sin\theta) $$

$$y=R(1-cos\theta) $$

$$t_{x_0}=\int_0^{x_0}{\frac{\sqrt{1+y^{'2}}}{\sqrt{\frac{3}{4} gy}}}dx=\int_0^{\theta}{\frac{\sqrt{(dx)^2+(dy)^2}}{\sqrt{\frac{3}{4}gy}}} $$

Processing molecules：

$$\sqrt{(dx)^2+(dy)^2}=d\theta\sqrt{(\frac{dx}{d\theta})^2+(\frac{dy}{d\theta})^2}=\sqrt{2}R\sqrt{1-cos\theta} $$

$$t(\theta)=\int_{0}^{\theta}\frac{\sqrt{2}R\sqrt{1-cos\theta}}{\sqrt{\frac{4}{3}}R\sqrt{1-cos\theta}}d\theta=\sqrt{\frac{3R}{2g}}\theta $$

Here, only the first half is taken, which is $\theta \in [0, \pi] $. For the second half, it is considered symmetrical to the first half. During the animation demonstration, $\theta $ is adjusted to be a function proportional to the frame rate

Assuming that the angle between the velocity direction of the disk and the seeding direction is $\alpha$, and the angle between the cycloid tangent of the center trajectory and the horizontal line is $\ gamma $, then the two are complementary to each other.Assuming the static sliding friction coefficient is $\mu$.Consider a downward cycloid as the center trajectory：

$$x=c(\theta-sin\theta) $$

$$y=-c(1-cos\theta) $$

Firstly, consider a feasible initial position where the velocity is zero, identify the critical condition for the disk to slide, and consider $f=\mu F_N$

$$mgsin\alpha=F_N $$

$$mgcos\alpha-f=ma $$

$$a=\beta R $$

$$fR=\frac{1}{2}mR^2\beta $$

obtain

$$tan\alpha=\frac{1}{3\mu} $$

The following needs to be solved using $\gamma$：

$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy}{d\theta}\frac{d\theta}{dx}=-\frac{sin\theta}{1-cos\theta}=-\frac{1}{tan\frac{\theta}{2}}=-tan\gamma=-\frac{1}{tan\alpha}=-3\mu $$

$$tan\frac{\theta}{2}=\frac{1}{3\mu} $$

$$\theta=2arctan\frac{1}{3\mu} $$

because of $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$，the non sliding range obtained is：

$$\theta \in [2arctan\frac{1}{3\mu},2\pi-2arctan\frac{1}{3\mu}] $$

Endless Journey

Rostky was born in an obscure little town, which was unprecedentedly prosperous when he was a child. Clean marble was used to build chess tables, and newborn piglets and lambs were given names by children or wandering poets. However, by the time he was twenty, all of this existed only in his memories. The chess tables had been dismantled and sold for money, and the remaining livestock could no longer provide for new life. Neighbors had no interest in celebrating any holidays, and most of the town’s casinos and taverns had closed down. People had nothing to talk about, and most of them couldn’t even use the Common Era to explain where they were in history. The most talked about topics were still the chickens, ducks, geese, and sheep at home. They would talk about how to raise these livestock, citing classical texts with solemn seriousness, from the veterinarian’s “General Principles of Animal Husbandry” to Aristotle’s treatise on irrational numbers; Today, there are few people left who still raise livestock at home, and most people no longer have the qualifications to engage in lively discussions at the tavern, instead returning home to engage in so-called idle speculation.
A wandering poet once composed a verse for this nameless small town: “Prosperous places are all alike, but each decaying one is decaying in its own way.” This sparked a craze at the time, and people gathered again in the tavern to discuss the uniqueness of this place’s decay. Some stinking old men brought up history dating back hundreds of years, and even the souls of the dead in the graveyard became their arguments. literary enthusiasts analyzed every word, with each character warranting pages of analysis and commentary. Rotszki emerged from this farce having left his childhood behind; he believed he had encountered his life mentor, the owner of the town’s only bookstore: Old Man McLeod. This old man had not left his dilapidated store since his youth, yet during that time he stayed out all night, passionate and enthusiastic, delivering impassioned speeches at the bar like a child, expressing his views. Rostky was immediately drawn to his flowing white hair, and years later, he still couldn’t forget the first thing he said when they met: “Your brain will one day shrink and disappear because it’s full of water.” At the time, he was yelling at a middle school student.
When everyone finally went home, Rostky became a regular at the bookstore. He believed that the old man’s words were completely different from those of others, and his logic was unique, like that of God.
When everyone finally went home, Rotsky became a regular at the bookstore. He believed that the old man’s words were completely different from those of other people, his logic was unique and omnipotent like God’s, and his vocabulary was as varied as that of the famous critic Goethe. Some say that the history of this store is longer than that of the village’s meteorite, and the owner even traces it back further; after all, he was born in a certain place and went on to establish this store. The old man just smiled and said nothing. The days in the bookstore are so leisurely that he has left his footprints in the messy piles of books. The Latin books written by the ancient Greek patriarch and the Chinese eunuch’s writings, hidden in the deepest corners, have been brought back to life because of him. He made a few close friends, all regulars at the bookstore. They often talked day and night, arguing endlessly over an idea or an article, as if the farce had never stopped, those lives had never grown old, that splendor had never faded, and that journey had never ended.
One day, one of his friends excitedly announced some news: he had won second prize in the lottery and received a ticket that could be used indefinitely. Rotsky traded his property and five pieces of gold for the ticket and boarded the train for an endless journey. He sat by the window; there were few people in the carriage. He saw the rapeseed flowers in full bloom in June; an old woman tending the fields shouted angrily at the train, waking her from her nap; female students returning to school posed by the pond, drawing frequent glances from passengers; The surrounding Milocca Forest was once again bypassed, with huge trees looming over the train tracks. Occasionally, someone would ask him where the final destination was, and he would reply, “Amipetis.” When would they arrive? Perhaps today, or yesterday; no one knew.
Years later, facing the Milocca Jungle, Roztki would recall that evening when he met Maile.
